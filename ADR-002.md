# ADR-002: Document/OCR layer — Docling + Granite-Docling, Qwen2.5-VL-72B fallback

| Status     | Accepted                                |
| ---------- | --------------------------------------- |
| Date       | 2026-05-18                              |
| Deciders   | Putsch Group Platform Engineering       |
| Supersedes | none                                    |
| Related    | ADR-001 (Stack: CrewAI + LangGraph)     |

## Context

The Putsch AP Crew ingests German B2B Eingangsrechnungen and must produce
structured fields for downstream agents (SAP 3-way match, DATEV journal
posting, master-data updates). Extraction accuracy is the multiplicative
gate on the entire stack's economics:

> If extraction is 90% accurate, downstream agents cap at 90%.
> At 99%, downstream can compound to near-zero exception rates.

The customer is a German Mittelstand under three concurrent regulatory
regimes: GDPR (data residency, redaction), the EU AI Act (model
governance, traceability), and Betriebsrat scrutiny (no opaque black
boxes for HR-adjacent processes). The chosen document layer must support
self-hosted air-gapped operation in Frankfurt.

We must also handle the realistic input distribution of a Mittelstand
AP department: ~30% clean XRechnung XML, ~50% digital PDFs from ERP
systems, ~15% scanned PDFs (some with handwritten margin annotations),
~5% photographed-on-a-phone reimbursement receipts. A single-model
approach will fail on the long tail.

## Decision

**Primary**: Docling + `ibm-granite/granite-docling-258M`.

**Fallback**: Qwen2.5-VL-72B-Instruct, self-hosted via vLLM in Frankfurt,
triggered per-field when Docling confidence is below threshold or
arithmetic/structural validation fails.

**Reconciliation**: Per-field confidence calibrator combining Docling
region scores, deterministic validator results (IBAN MOD-97, USt-IdNr
format, `netto + mwst = brutto`), and a secondary LLM-as-judge pass on
critical fields. Per-field winner-take-the-higher-confidence merge,
re-validated through the schema.

## Why Docling (primary)

1. **Governance**. IBM Research authored it; LF AI & Data Foundation
   governs it; Red Hat ships it in RHEL AI. This is infrastructure with
   the same backing as Kubernetes or Postgres, not a startup with a 12-
   month runway.
2. **License**. MIT. No license surprises in year three of an enterprise
   deployment.
3. **TableFormer**. Independent benchmark (Procycons, 2025) on German
   B2B documents shows 97.9% cell-level table accuracy, beating
   Unstructured.io and LlamaParse on Bayer annual reports. For
   Eingangsrechnungen with line-item tables, this is the single most
   important capability.
4. **Air-gapped**. Granite-Docling 258M fits on a single L4 GPU and
   runs at 0.49s/page. Model artifacts are downloadable once and pinned
   inside the container. No outbound model traffic at runtime.
5. **Native multilingual**. German is first-class — not a translation
   afterthought.
6. **Native integrations** with CrewAI, LangChain, LlamaIndex,
   Haystack. Reduces glue code we don't have to maintain.

## Why Qwen2.5-VL-72B (fallback)

The fallback is the production-grade differentiator. Docling alone is
~92% F1 on clean German B2B PDFs but drops sharply on:

- Scans with skew or low DPI (>20% character error from OCR).
- Handwritten annotations in margins.
- Eingangsstempel partially overlapping with structured fields.
- Unusual layouts from older Mittelstand vendors whose templates the
  pretraining corpus did not see.

Qwen2.5-VL-72B is a vision-language reasoner: it sees the page as an
image and answers questions about its content. Its failure modes are
**uncorrelated** with Docling's:

| Failure mode                  | Docling     | Qwen-VL     |
| ----------------------------- | ----------- | ----------- |
| Clean digital PDF             | strong      | strong      |
| Dense numerical table         | strong      | medium      |
| Low-DPI scan                  | medium      | strong      |
| Handwritten margin note       | weak        | strong      |
| Overlapping stamp             | weak        | strong      |
| Unusual single-column layout  | weak        | strong      |

The fallback's value is precisely in this uncorrelation. Stacking two
~92% F1 models with the same failure mode gives ~92% F1. Stacking two
~92% F1 models with uncorrelated failure modes — even imperfectly —
approaches the union of their strengths, which our internal benchmarks
suggest is ~98–99% F1 on the realistic Putsch input distribution.

### Why Qwen2.5-VL-72B specifically, not a smaller VLM

- 72B is the smallest VLM that maintains strong German document
  understanding under JPEG compression artifacts and Eingangsstempel
  overlap. Smaller VLMs (LLaVA-7B, Idefics3-8B, Pixtral-12B) miss
  Lieferantennummer or Bestellnummer when they are typeset inside a
  table cell that's also intersected by a stamp.
- Self-hostable: open weights under Apache 2.0. Runs on the same
  vLLM infrastructure we already operate for non-document agents.
- EU residency: no inference traffic leaves Frankfurt.

### Why **not** Mistral OCR for fallback

Mistral OCR is API-only, hosted in Paris. It is excellent and would be
a defensible choice. We chose Qwen2.5-VL-72B self-hosted because:

1. Eingangsrechnungen contain IBAN, USt-IdNr, Lieferantennummer, and
   sometimes Steuernummer of natural persons (sole-trader vendors).
   Avoiding any third-party inference path, even an EU one, narrows our
   GDPR DPIA scope and simplifies the Betriebsrat conversation.
2. We are already paying for vLLM GPU capacity for the rest of the
   stack. Marginal cost of running Qwen-VL on it is GPU-hours, not
   per-token API fees.
3. Vendor diversification: Mistral is already our primary extraction
   LLM (Mistral Large via La Plateforme for the DSPy program). Adding
   them as the OCR fallback concentrates risk on one vendor.

## Why **not** the alternatives

### Unstructured.io
- Weaker table extraction on German enterprise documents (Procycons
  benchmark: 89.4% vs Docling's 97.9% on Bayer annual reports).
- OSS edition is meaningfully less capable than the hosted version,
  and the hosted version is US-hosted.
- The license model has shifted twice in 18 months. We avoid layer-
  zero infrastructure with that vendor risk.

### LlamaParse
- Vendor API. Documents are sent to LlamaIndex-hosted inference.
- EU data residency is documented as "best effort" rather than
  contractual. GDPR DPIA fails.
- Table accuracy: 91% on the same benchmark. Worse than Docling.

### ABBYY FineReader / FlexiCapture
- Closed-source. License cost scales per-document; at Putsch's
  volume that's a six-figure annual line item.
- Does not compose with our self-hosted agentic stack. Every
  integration is glue code we'd own forever.
- The model is upgraded by ABBYY on their schedule, not ours. We
  cannot pin a version to a Langfuse-tracked eval run.

### MinerU
- Apache 2.0. Faster (0.21s/page on GPU vs Docling's 0.49s/page).
- Better on academic-style layouts (math, diagrams).
- Worse on enterprise documents: TableFormer's column-spanning logic
  consistently beats MinerU on multi-row header tables, which is
  exactly what German B2B invoices use.
- We keep MinerU on the watch list for future Lieferschein/Bestellung
  modules where the document layout is more like a manual than an
  invoice.

### Pure Mistral Large / GPT-4o with PDF tools
- Stochastic. No deterministic table structure, no per-region scores.
- Audit replay is hard: re-running the same prompt against the same
  document is not bit-stable. Docling's structural pass is.
- Higher per-document cost at our volume.

## Calibration methodology

The confidence calibrator is the single most-strategic piece of code in
this module. It combines four signals:

1. **Docling region confidence**: Docling's self-reported per-element
   score, mapped to schema fields via an explicit label map (kept
   auditable, not learned).
2. **Deterministic validator**: IBAN MOD-97, USt-IdNr country format +
   checksum, German tax number format, date plausibility, MwSt rate
   plausibility. Cheap, near-zero false positive rate, near-fatal
   negative signal.
3. **Structure consistency**: `netto + mwst = brutto` within ±2 cents;
   line-item totals sum to netto; Leistungsdatum within ±30 days of
   Rechnungsdatum. Violation collapses confidence on all amount fields
   simultaneously — these errors don't occur in isolation.
4. **LLM-as-judge**: secondary pass on critical fields only (USt-IdNr,
   IBAN, amounts, dates). Cheap; uses a smaller LLM (Mistral Medium) so
   per-invoice judge cost stays under one cent.

Final per-field score is the **min** of these signals, not their average.
Any single negative signal collapses confidence to that signal's level.
Averaging hides hard failures behind weak positives — the opposite of
what we want when an error costs a 4-eyes review or a duplicate vendor
record.

Thresholds:
- `fallback_threshold = 0.85` — below this, route through Qwen-VL.
- `critical_field_threshold = 0.90` — below this even after fallback,
  refuse to ship; raise `ConfidenceError` into the AP Crew's HITL queue.

Both thresholds are env-tunable. Distribution shift detected via
Langfuse — when overall-min drops, the threshold gets retuned, not
the calibrator logic.

## Future maintainers: do not remove the fallback

When Granite-Docling 1B (or 7B) ships and benchmarks beat 99% F1 on
clean PDFs, you will be tempted to remove the fallback. **Do not.**

The fallback exists because Docling and Qwen-VL have uncorrelated
failure modes, not because Docling is bad. Even if Docling reaches 99%
F1 on the clean PDF distribution, it will still be ~92% on
handwritten-annotated scans, because that's a distributional
weakness, not a model-quality weakness. Removing the fallback uncaps
the entire stack's exception rate at Docling's worst-case input
distribution.

When you do upgrade Docling, the appropriate move is to **re-tune the
fallback threshold downward** so fallback fires less often, not remove
it. Use the Langfuse dataset run history (`putsch_docs_eval_YYYYMM`) to
pick the new threshold from data.

## Consequences

**Positive**
- Production-grade accuracy on real-world German Mittelstand
  invoices, not just clean XRechnung XML.
- Self-hosted, air-gapped, EU data residency by default.
- Auditable: per-field provenance recorded in ExtractionTrace; every
  model decision logged to Langfuse.
- Composable: same extractor served as CrewAI tool and MCP tool, so
  non-CrewAI consumers (LangGraph nodes, future integrations) get the
  same contract.

**Negative**
- Operational complexity: two model runtimes (Docling + vLLM) instead
  of one.
- Latency: fallback invocation adds 10-30 seconds per document.
  Mitigated by per-field triggering — fallback fires only when needed.
- GPU footprint: 72B Qwen-VL is heavy. Justified by the uncorrelated-
  failure-mode argument; would not be justified for a smaller-volume
  customer.

**Mitigations**
- Circuit breaker on vLLM endpoint so sustained outages degrade
  gracefully rather than melting the AP Crew's latency budget.
- Per-field merge so we only fall back on the regions that need it,
  not the whole document.
- LLM-as-judge bounded to critical fields so per-invoice judge cost
  stays under one cent.
